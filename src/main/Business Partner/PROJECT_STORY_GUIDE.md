## 🎯 Project Story Framework

Your story should have these 5 components:

### 1. SITUATION (1-2 min)
**Tell them:**
- What was the business problem?
- Who were involved? (PM, Ops, Analytics, etc.)
- Why was this important?
- What was the timeline?

**Good Example:**
```
"I was working with the Dasher operations team. They were struggling
to optimize Dasher scheduling. PMs wanted to understand peak hours by
geography. Ops wanted to predict demand to schedule drivers efficiently.

The challenge: no unified view of demand patterns. Data was scattered
across multiple systems. This was slowing down decision-making by a week
each time they needed analysis."
```

**Bad Example:**
```
"I worked on a big data pipeline project"
```

---

### 2. YOUR CHALLENGE (1-2 min)
**Tell them:**
- What was hard about working cross-functionally?
- What conflicts or misalignments existed?
- What constraints did you face?
- What did stakeholders want?

**Good Example:**
```
"The real challenge was: PMs wanted real-time dashboards (nice-to-have),
but Ops needed accurate daily forecasts (must-have). The source data was
inconsistent. Dashers gave their location intermittently. Restaurants
updated their busy times manually.

Plus, I had to explain technical data issues to non-technical ops team.
Some wanted immediate fix, others wanted perfect solution. And I had 3
weeks to deliver."
```

**Bad Example:**
```
"The data quality was bad"
```

---

### 3. YOUR APPROACH (2-3 min)
**Tell them:**
- How did you understand the problem?
- How did you communicate?
- What was your process?
- How did you handle feedback?

**Good Example:**
```
"I started by interviewing both teams separately, then together.
Key insight: Ops' 'perfect' solution would take months. PMs' 'real-time'
didn't exist yet in the data. So I proposed an MVP:

1. Daily batch pipeline for Ops (80% of need, 2 weeks effort)
2. Simple dashboard for PMs (visible progress, iterable)
3. Data quality monitoring (catch issues early)

I met with each team weekly:
- Ops: Showed progress on their metrics
- PMs: Showed early dashboard drafts
- Both: Shared a weekly status document

When requirements changed mid-way (Ops suddenly needed location data),
I showed them the impact (3 more weeks), and we negotiated a simpler
version that was good enough."
```

**Bad Example:**
```
"I built the pipeline and delivered it"
```

---

### 4. YOUR OUTCOME (1 min)
**Tell them:**
- What did you deliver?
- What was the business impact?
- What metrics improved?
- What did stakeholders say?

**Good Example:**
```
"We delivered a daily pipeline + dashboard in 3 weeks.
Within a month, Ops used it to schedule 15% more efficiently.
They went from 1-week analysis cycles to 1-day cycles.

PMs got their visibility. Ops got their automation.
One team lead told me: 'This data work is now something I rely on
and trust every day.'"
```

**Bad Example:**
```
"We delivered the pipeline"
```

---

### 5. YOUR LEARNING (1 min)
**Tell them:**
- What would you do differently?
- What did you learn about cross-functional work?
- How did this shape you?

**Good Example:**
```
"I learned that starting with what's 'good enough' instead of 'perfect'
builds trust. When I showed Ops value in 2 weeks, they became evangelists.

I also learned that frequent communication prevents surprises.
When I communicated the mid-way change, they were prepared.

If I did it again, I'd spend even more time upfront understanding
each team's actual constraints vs what they asked for."
```

**Bad Example:**
```
"I learned Spark is useful"
```

## 🎯 Good Project Characteristics

Your project story should have:

✅ **A real business problem** (not internal engineering optimization)  
✅ **Multiple stakeholders** (at least 2 teams)  
✅ **A communication challenge** (had to explain to non-technical people)  
✅ **Ambiguity that you had to navigate** (not a straightforward requirement)  
✅ **A tradeoff you had to make** (couldn't do everything)  
✅ **Feedback you incorporated** (changed based on input)  
✅ **Business impact** (can show how it helped)  
✅ **Something you learned** (reflection on collaboration)  

---

## ❌ Projects to AVOID

**Don't pick a project where:**
- You worked alone (no collaboration)
- Requirements were crystal clear (no ambiguity)
- It was pure engineering optimization (no business connection)
- You had full control and no one questioned you (no feedback)
- You can't explain the business impact (no business sense)
- You blame others for issues (not collaborative)
- It failed and you don't have learning (no reflection)

## 📝 Example Project Stories

### Example 1: Data Quality Project

```
SITUATION:
We were losing customers because cold food complaints were increasing.
The product team wanted to track food temperature. Operations wanted to
know which merchants were the problem. Analytics wanted dashboards.

CHALLENGE:
Three different needs. No existing temperature data. Merchants weren't
tracking it consistently. I had to help them see the value before they'd
invest in sensors. 2-week deadline (product review coming up).

APPROACH:
I met with each team:
- Product: Showed we could track with existing delivery data
- Ops: Proposed pilot with 20 merchants to validate value
- Analytics: Designed a schema that could grow later

I explained to Product Manager: "We can't get real temperature data yet,
but we can infer it from delivery time + customer ratings. Let's validate
this works before investing in hardware."

When Ops said "we need perfect accuracy," I negotiated: "Let's start with
good enough. If we can prevent 30% of complaints, that justifies hardware
investment."

OUTCOME:
We got 20 merchants with better data collection. In 2 months, complaints
dropped 25%. This became a case study for rolling out to all merchants.

LEARNING:
I learned that "good enough to validate" beats "perfect but delayed."
Getting data from 20 merchants fast taught me more than waiting for 100.
```

### Example 2: OpenStreetMap Data Quality Optimization (YOUR REAL PROJECT) ⭐

```
SITUATION:
At Unify Technologies (Apple Maps team), we were ingesting massive data
from OpenStreetMap (OSM) - a crowdsourced geospatial dataset used by millions
of users. The problem: data quality was inconsistent, causing real user issues.
Wrong coordinates meant wrong locations on maps, missing data meant incomplete
information, duplicates meant confusing experiences.

The stakeholders involved:
- Product team: Wanted accurate maps for good user experience  
- Analytics team: Needed clean, reliable data for insights
- Platform team: Concerned about processing costs and pipeline performance
- Data Science: Building location-based features needed high-quality inputs

The business impact was significant: Poor data quality → Bad user experience
→ Lower engagement on Apple Maps. This was pre-iOS release, so we had a
tight 3-week window to fix it.

CHALLENGE:
This was genuinely complex because I had to balance multiple, often conflicting
needs:

1. DATA QUALITY vs SPEED
   - Analytics insisted on perfection: "Validate every field, cross-reference
     everything"
   - But Product was in crisis mode: "The iOS release is in 3 weeks. We need
     good data FAST, not perfect data late"
   - Platform chimed in: "Complex validation is expensive. We can't afford
     that much compute"
   - I had to figure out: What's the 80/20 rule? What matters most?

2. COST vs ACCURACY
   - Platform team: "Thorough validation costs millions in compute. Use cheap
     validation only"
   - Analytics: "Bad data is worse than expensive validation. Do it right"
   - Finance: "We need it cheap AND accurate. Pick one."
   - I had to understand: Which validations actually prevent real user issues?
     Which are nice-to-have?

3. COMMUNICATION COMPLEXITY
   - Product team didn't understand technical data quality issues
   - Platform team didn't understand business urgency
   - Analytics wanted technical perfection but couldn't articulate WHY
   - I was translating between 3 different languages and priorities

The core tension: OSM is crowdsourced data → inherently messy and inconsistent.
How do we clean it efficiently without spending millions on compute, while still
meeting the iOS deadline, and satisfying everyone's needs?

APPROACH:
I didn't jump to coding. I started with questions and collaboration:

1. UNDERSTAND EACH TEAM'S REAL NEEDS (Not what they said, but why)
   
   Meeting 1 - Product: "What specific data quality issues hurt users most?"
   - Their answer: "Wrong coordinates (drives people to wrong places), 
     missing street names (can't find the place), duplicates (confusing UI)"
   - My insight: Not ALL fields matter equally
   
   Meeting 2 - Platform: "What validation is most expensive to run?"
   - Their answer: "Cross-referencing against multiple data sources. That's
     complex SQL that requires joining huge tables"
   - My insight: We can do cheap validations first, expensive ones later
   
   Meeting 3 - Analytics: "For the features you're building, what's 'good
     enough' accuracy?"
   - Their answer: "95% accuracy is excellent. 100% is nice but takes 3x
     longer to compute"
   - My insight: We're chasing perfection when good is enough

2. PROPOSE PHASED MVP APPROACH (Risk, not waterfall)
   
   I suggested: "Let's implement validation in phases, releasing value early:
   
   Phase 1 (Week 1): Basic Validation - Focus on CHEAP wins
   ├─ Check for NULL values in critical fields
   ├─ Validate coordinate ranges (latitude -90 to 90, longitude -180 to 180)
   ├─ Remove exact duplicates (same coordinates + name)
   ├─ Expected outcome: 80% accuracy improvement, 15% faster data consumption
   └─ Cost: Minimal compute
   
   Phase 2 (Week 2): Smart Validation + Optimization - Incremental approach
   ├─ Implement CDC (Change Data Capture) for incremental processing
   │  └─ Why: Only process CHANGED data, not entire OSM dataset each time
   │  └─ Benefit: 50% reduction in processing time
   ├─ Add validation for street name formatting/standardization
   ├─ Implement parallel Spark jobs on EMR for efficiency
   └─ Expected outcome: 95% accuracy, 50% faster pipeline
   
   Phase 3 (Week 3+): Cost Optimization - Profile and optimize
   ├─ Deep profiling of Spark jobs to identify bottlenecks
   ├─ Optimize slow-running transformations
   ├─ Reduce memory usage and resource allocation
   └─ Expected outcome: 35% reduction in cloud resource costs"
   
   I explained in PLAIN ENGLISH (not jargon):
   
   "We could spend 3 weeks perfecting everything and miss the iOS deadline.
   OR we can do this smart way:
   
   Week 1: Get to 80% accuracy with cheap, easy validations. Launch.
   Week 2: Get to 95% with smarter validations + optimization. Launch.
   Week 3: If issues arise, fix them. If not, optimize costs.
   
   Risk: Week 1 users see 80% accuracy (better than the broken 50% they have
   now). Week 2 improves it. We never miss the deadline.
   
   This is better than 'do nothing until perfect,' which means iOS ships with
   broken data."

3. COMMUNICATE & SHOW PROGRESS
   
   Weekly meetings with each stakeholder:
   - Product: "Here are the accuracy metrics improving week-over-week"
     └─ Showed dashboard: Week 1 → 80%, Week 2 → 95%
   - Platform: "Here's how much compute we're saving"
     └─ Showed: Resource costs down, processing time improved
   - Analytics: "Here's data quality improving for your features"
     └─ Showed: Validation checks catching issues proactively

4. HANDLE SCOPE CHANGES GRACEFULLY
   
   MID-PROJECT: iOS release timeline got delayed (actually good news!)
   
   What could have happened: "Great, now I can do all 3 phases slowly"
   
   What I actually did:
   - ASKED: "Does this change your priorities? What should I focus on now?"
   - Product said: "Actually, now we have time. Can you do Phase 2?"
   - I said: "Yes, and here's the plan to deliver it in the original Week 2"
   - This showed flexibility and strategic thinking, not just following orders

OUTCOME:
We delivered results that EXCEEDED expectations:

✅ DATA QUALITY IMPROVEMENTS
   - Improved data accuracy by 20% through rigorous validation processes
   - Cleaned duplicate entries from OSM (crowdsourced = lots of duplicates)
   - Validated coordinate ranges and street names
   - Product team could NOW trust the data for critical features

✅ PERFORMANCE OPTIMIZATION
   - Optimized Spark jobs on EMR platforms
   - Reduced job completion time for data mutators by 50%
   - Pipeline went from 6+ hours to 3 hours
   - Analytics could NOW iterate faster on their work

✅ COST EFFICIENCY
   - Reduced resource costs by 35% through Spark optimization + CDC
   - Implemented Change Data Capture for incremental updates
   - Platform team got budget back for other projects
   - Finance approved further investment in data quality

✅ BUSINESS & TEAM IMPACT
   - iOS release shipped with accurate map data
   - User feedback: "Maps is more reliable now"
   - Data Science could build better location features
   - One Product Manager later told me: "Your data ingestion is the most
     trusted part of our entire pipeline now"

LEARNING:
Three insights profoundly shaped how I work:

1. "Ask WHY before building WHAT"
   - Don't assume requirements. They often don't tell you the truth
   - Product said "perfect validation" but really needed 95% accuracy
   - Platform said "keep costs down" but really needed predictable performance
   - By asking, I solved the REAL problem, not the stated problem
   - Now I ALWAYS start with questions, not with my technical solution

2. "Good Enough + Fast > Perfect + Late"
   - Phase 1 gave value in Week 1, built trust immediately
   - This trust made Week 2 and 3 smoother because stakeholders believed in me
   - Over-engineering for perfection is often NOT the solution
   - Speed of delivery matters more than theoretical elegance
   - This taught me to think MVP in everything I do

3. "The hard part isn't the code, it's the COMMUNICATION"
   - The technical solution (CDC + Spark optimization) was actually
     straightforward - standard data engineering
   - The HARD part was:
     └─ Explaining why Phase 1 before Phase 2
     └─ Showing Platform how Phase 2 SAVED them money
     └─ Helping Analytics understand why 95% > 100%
     └─ Translating between 3 different teams' needs
   - Now I spend more time on communication than code

If I did this project again:
- I'd spend EVEN MORE time upfront in conversations with each team
- I'd create a SHARED metrics dashboard so everyone saw progress together
- I'd prepare for scope creep and requirement changes BEFORE they happen
- I'd document assumptions and decisions for future reference

This project taught me something crucial: Data engineering isn't JUST about
writing code. It's about understanding business needs, technical constraints,
and translating between teams who don't speak the same language. The best
pipeline isn't the most sophisticated or technically elegant one. It's the
one that SOLVES REAL PROBLEMS for REAL PEOPLE, on TIME, within budget.
```

### Example 3: Pipeline Redesign Project

```
SITUATION:
Analytics team was frustrated. Data pipelines took 6 hours. They wanted
1-hour latency. Engineering said 1-hour was impossible with current design.
Finance cared about costs. Ops wanted reliable data above all.

CHALLENGE:
Every team wanted something different:
- Analytics: Faster (1-hour)
- Engineering: Cost-effective
- Ops: Reliable
- Finance: Cheap

The current pipeline was brittle. We spent 30% time fixing failures.
I had 4 weeks to redesign with a small team.

APPROACH:
I didn't start coding. I started asking:
- "Why do you need 1-hour?" → They actually needed 2-hour in many cases
- "What breaks most often?" → This one step failed 15% of the time
- "What costs the most?" → The most-used data was also most-expensive

I proposed: Fix the reliability issues first (this was cheaper), then
optimize the bottleneck (the 6-hour was due to one query).

Result: 3-hour latency (60% better), 40% cost reduction, 99% reliability.

OUTCOME:
Analytics loved it. Engineering got time for tech debt. Finance got cost
savings. We delivered faster than the original 1-hour ask would have taken.

LEARNING:
Understanding the "why" is critical. They said they needed 1-hour, but
really needed reliability and lower costs. By asking good questions,
I solved the real problem.
```

## 🚨 Things to Avoid in Your Story

❌ **Blaming others** - "The PM was wrong" or "Ops didn't understand"
- Instead: "I had to help them understand why..."

❌ **Too much technical jargon** - "We optimized the ETL with incremental processing"
- Instead: "We made queries 3x faster"

❌ **No business impact** - "I built a pipeline"
- Instead: "This improved decision-making speed by 80%"

❌ **Rigid thinking** - "We did it my way"
- Instead: "I iterated based on their feedback"

❌ **No reflection** - Just the facts
- Instead: "Here's what I learned about collaboration..."

❌ **Too long** - Takes more than 6 minutes
- Should be concise, impactful
